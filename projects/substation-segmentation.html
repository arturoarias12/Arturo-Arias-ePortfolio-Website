Power Substation Segmentation and New Substation Build Recommendations

A two-phase AI pipeline, developed by a team of four members for CMU's Introduction to Artificial Intelligence course, to identify underserved areas of the US power grid: a U-Net segmentation model trained on 0.6 m-resolution aerial imagery to locate existing substations, then a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming optimizer to recommend 50 new build sites where coverage gaps or demand density justify new infrastructure.

Problem and context

The US power grid is aging, and underserved areas face higher outage risk and longer restoration times. Identifying where new substations would provide the greatest coverage benefit requires two things: an accurate inventory of where substations currently exist, and a principled method for evaluating where new ones would have the most impact. Manual inventory from satellite imagery is time-consuming and inconsistent at scale.

This project automated both steps. Phase 1 applied computer vision to detect existing substations from aerial imagery. Phase 2 used those detections as the baseline for an optimization model that recommended 50 new build sites across Maricopa County, Arizona, ranked by coverage gap and demand density. The result is a replicable, data-driven framework for infrastructure planning that generalizes beyond the pilot geography.

Phase 1: Substation detection

The detection model uses a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-34 encoder, applied to four-band NAIP (National Agriculture Imagery Program) aerial imagery at 0.6 m/pixel resolution in 512×512-pixel patches. Ground-truth substation locations were sourced from OpenStreetMap and rasterized into binary segmentation masks used as training labels.

The dataset covers 10 US counties, yielding approximately 4,000 labeled patches. Training used 5-fold cross-validation to reduce variance across geographically heterogeneous patches; the five fold-specific checkpoints were ensembled at inference time by averaging their predicted probability maps. A custom scoring function ranked candidate locations using the top 1% of pixel probability mass per patch, filtering low-confidence detections and surfacing only high-confidence substation candidates.

Data augmentation (random flips, rotations, color jitter on the RGB channels) addressed overfitting on the geographically limited training set and improved generalization to the held-out test counties.

Each patch score is compared against an operating threshold to decide whether the patch contains a substation. Threshold selection was treated as a calibrated operating-point decision rather than a fixed architectural property: the automatically selected validation threshold proved too conservative on the held-out geography, so a manual sensitivity sweep identified 0.03 as the operating point that best balanced recall and precision. This threshold is not a universal constant; in a real planning workflow, the right value depends on the relative cost of false negatives (missed substations) versus false positives (spurious detections).

Why U-Net for substation detection?

Substations are small, irregular objects that occupy only a fraction of a typical aerial patch. U-Net's encoder-decoder structure with skip connections preserves fine spatial detail at multiple scales, making it well suited for segmenting compact infrastructure features that would be lost in a classification-only approach. The ResNet-34 encoder provides a strong, pretrained feature backbone without the memory cost of deeper architectures on 512-pixel patches.

Phase 2: Site recommendations

As an academic proof of concept, Phase 2 took the ensemble's predicted substation map as the supply baseline and framed new-site selection as a p-median facility location problem, solved with a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming model using the PuLP library and the HiGHS solver. The outputs are intended as a planning support tool to illustrate the approach, not as real infrastructure decisions.

Demand was estimated by overlaying US Census TIGER block-group data with Microsoft Building Footprints to produce a fine-grained residential demand grid across Maricopa County. Candidate sites were filtered for suitability using OpenStreetMap land-use tags (excluding protected areas, water bodies, and dense urban cores) and validated against existing OSM transmission line proximity. Reverse geocoding via Nominatim and spatial queries via the Overpass API enriched each candidate with address and zoning context.

The optimizer solved for 50 new sites subject to a constraint that no recommended site overlaps an existing substation within a 500 m exclusion radius. Two objective modes were implemented: minimize total weighted distance from demand cells to their nearest substation, and maximize coverage for demand cells currently more than 10 km from any substation. The final set of 50 sites blends both objectives, with 19 sites selected primarily for distance reduction and 31 for servicing high-demand cells with inadequate nearby coverage.

Results

At operating threshold 0.03, the 5-fold ensemble achieved a precision of 0.975 and ROC-AUC of 0.945 on the held-out test set. The high precision confirms that positive detections are reliable; the ROC-AUC confirms strong ranking ability on unseen geography, validating that the model generalizes beyond its training counties. Detection recall was the primary gap, driven by small or partially occluded substations in dense patches.

In the Central Phoenix optimization pilot, incorporating the 50 recommended sites reduced mean service distance from 1,306 m to 266 m, an 80% reduction, and cut the share of demand cells more than 10 km from any substation from 11.6% to 4.1%. These figures demonstrate that even a relatively small number of well-placed new substations can substantially improve coverage at a regional scale.

Future work

Improving detection recall on small and partially occluded substations is the most direct extension, and could be addressed through multi-scale feature aggregation or targeted augmentation for low-visibility patches. Replacing building-count density with actual energy consumption records would produce a more accurate demand grid, particularly in industrial and commercial areas where residential proxies underestimate load. Adding transmission line capacity constraints and construction cost modeling to the MILP formulation would bring the optimization step meaningfully closer to real planning practice. Finally, developing a more generalizable labeling workflow (for instance, through semi-supervised or weakly supervised approaches) would reduce the cost of extending the framework to new geographies without requiring full OSM re-annotation.
