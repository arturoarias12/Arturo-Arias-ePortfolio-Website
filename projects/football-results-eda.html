EDA of International Football Results (1872–2024)

A solo exploratory data analysis of 47,399 international football match records spanning 152 years, examining team performance trends, home advantage effects, goal-scoring patterns, and classic regional rivalries through custom Pandas aggregations and Seaborn visualizations, published as a Kaggle notebook.

Dataset overview

The analysis is built on a public dataset by Mart Jürisoo covering men's full international matches from November 30, 1872 through UEFA Euro 2024 and Copa América 2024. It contains nine fields: date, home team, away team, home and away scores (including extra time, excluding penalty shootouts), tournament name, host city, host country, and a neutral venue flag. The dataset has no missing values and requires only whitespace trimming and a datetime conversion before analysis.

The 47,399 records span 327 unique home-side names across 2,064 host cities and 270 countries. Friendly matches account for roughly 38% of all records; the remainder covers FIFA World Cup qualifiers, regional championships (UEFA Euro, Copa América, African Cup of Nations, AFC Asian Cup, CONCACAF Gold Cup), and other official competitions. The dataset strictly excludes Olympic Games, B-team matches, U-23 fixtures, and league select games.

As part of a prior project, I contributed verified historical results for Peru to this dataset, which is now reflected in the Kaggle discussion thread.

Analysis

Following a standard EDA structure (description, cleaning, statistical summary, exploratory visualizations, and insights), the notebook addresses ten questions through custom Pandas aggregations, filtered DataFrames, and Seaborn bar plots and histograms:

  1. Which teams have the best historical performance overall?
  2. Which team has conceded the highest number of goals?
  3. Among Brazil, Argentina, and Uruguay, who performs best head-to-head?
  4. Which cities have hosted the most matches in history?
  5. Which team has had the best performance since 2003?
  6. Does hosting a major tournament improve a country's results in that tournament?
  7. Are there teams that perform better away than at home?
  8. Among Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Paraguay, and Peru, who is the "fourth big" of South America?
  9. What are the five most historically repeated international matches?
  10. Which teams average the most goals scored per match?

Performance comparisons filter for teams with at least 100 matches played and at least one fixture since 2007, avoiding distortion from nations that no longer exist or from teams with very few matches.

Key findings

Scoring patterns and home advantage

Across all 47,399 matches, home teams average significantly more goals than visiting sides. The 0.58-goal gap is a consistent, data-backed measure of home advantage across 152 years of international competition.

The Big Three of South America: head-to-head

Filtering to matches exclusively among Brazil, Argentina, and Uruguay, Brazil holds a narrow lead over Argentina. The gap between the two is just 0.4 percentage points across hundreds of fixtures, confirming one of football's most fiercely contested rivalries. Uruguay trails by a wider margin at 37.1%.

Most repeated international fixtures

El Clásico del Río de la Plata (Argentina vs. Uruguay) is the most repeated international fixture in recorded history at 182 matches. The table also includes a surprising entry: Guernsey vs. Jersey, a rivalry between two British Crown dependencies, ranks fourth, ahead of the historic England vs. Scotland fixture.

Other notable findings

Host countries win significantly more matches than non-hosts in the same major competitions, providing data-backed confirmation of home-tournament advantage. In the "fourth big of South America" debate among Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Paraguay, and Peru, the result depends on the measure applied: Colombia leads in overall performance at 49.5% across all their matches, while Chile leads in direct head-to-head results against the other four, making the debate most compelling between those two nations. A geographic surprise: Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia is the city with the most international matches hosted in history, a result that holds even after filtering out friendlies.

On the performance metric

Performance is calculated as points earned divided by the maximum possible points (3 per match), expressed as a percentage. This accounts for both wins and draws across varying match counts, making it a more consistent basis for comparison than raw wins or total points.
