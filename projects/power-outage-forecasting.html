Power Outage Forecasting and Emergency Generator Allocation

An end-to-end ML pipeline, developed by a four-person team, for predicting county-level power outages across Michigan's 83 counties at 24h and 48h horizons, comparing SARIMA, LSTM, and two-stage hurdle models on highly zero-inflated data, then using the best model's predictions to pre-position five emergency generators across the state.

Problem and context

Extreme weather events (thunderstorms, high winds, heat waves) are primary drivers of large-scale power outages across the United States. These disruptions impose substantial social and economic costs, especially on vulnerable populations who depend on medical equipment or cannot afford extended disruptions. Emergency managers need to pre-position generators before a storm arrives, which requires reliable short-horizon forecasts at a granular geographic level.

This project addressed two linked tasks: (1) predict hourly outage counts for each of Michigan's 83 counties over 24h and 48h horizons, using only past observations and weather data available at forecast time; and (2) use those predictions to decide how to allocate five backup generators, each capable of supporting up to 1,000 households, which cannot be relocated once deployed.

Data

The dataset is in NetCDF format and contains county-level hourly observations for Michigan from April 1 to June 30, 2023, covering 83 counties across 2,161 hourly timestamps. Each record includes an outage count (out), 109 weather and environmental variables, and a tracked variable serving as a proxy for the number of households affected per county.

The outage variable is highly zero-inflated and right-skewed: roughly 70% of observations are zero and the median is 0, yet individual counties can reach thousands of affected customers during a storm event. This distribution makes modeling difficult: a model can achieve reasonable RMSE by predicting near-zero on most observations, yet still fail badly on the rare storm-event spikes that matter most for emergency response.

Weather features show high pairwise correlations, with several all-zero variables providing no signal. The highest single-feature correlation with outage is around 0.12, confirming that outage prediction requires combinations of variables rather than any single predictor.

Modeling approach

Four model families were implemented and compared against an all-zero baseline (reasonable given the zero inflation):

SARIMA and SARIMAX

SARIMA captures seasonal and autoregressive temporal structure in outage counts. Models were fit county-by-county with hyperparameter search over order parameters, with tuned configurations applied to the 20 counties with the highest validation error. SARIMAX extends SARIMA with exogenous weather features, tested in two variants: all weather reduced via PCA to 10 components, and the top-10 most correlated weather features. SARIMAX performed worse than baseline, suggesting the high-dimensional and noisy weather features hurt rather than helped in this linear framework.

LSTM

Three LSTM variants were built with a shared three-layer architecture (hidden dimension 128, dropout 0.3), trained on sliding windows of length 24 across all 83 counties using the Adam optimizer over 20 epochs:

  • LSTM-Base: outage count only (dim = 1)
  • LSTM-Simple: top-10 weather features by Pearson correlation, plus cyclic time encodings for hour-of-day and day-of-week (dim = 17)
  • LSTM-Full: all 77 statistically valid weather features (dim = 84)

All variants include lagged autoregressive inputs and use z-score normalization on the training split. The MLP head generates multi-step forecasts directly for the full H-step horizon (H = 24 or 48).

Two-stage hurdle model

Because outage counts are highly zero-inflated, a hurdle-style two-stage approach separates the problem into classification (will an outage occur?) and regression (how large will it be?). Stage 1 uses Elastic Net Logistic Regression and XGBoost Classifier; Stage 2 uses Elastic Net Regression and XGBoost Regressor trained only on non-zero observations, with log(1 + outage) as the target. The final prediction multiplies the estimated occurrence probability by the conditional magnitude estimate.

Results

Performance was evaluated using average RMSE across all 83 counties. Multiple model variants were tested across both horizons; the best results were RMSE 80.5 at 24h (SARIMA, tuned with seasonal structure) and RMSE 64.4 at 48h (LSTM-Simple, combining outage history, top-10 weather features, and cyclic time encodings). Both improved meaningfully on the all-zero baseline, which scored 100.3 at 24h and 75.4 at 48h. Full results across all model variants and configurations are reported in the project paper.

SARIMA's strength at the 24h horizon confirms that recent temporal patterns are the dominant signal for short-range forecasting. LSTM-Simple's advantage at 48h reflects the value of incorporating weather context as the forecast window extends. SARIMAX degraded below baseline, as high-dimensional weather features introduced noise rather than signal in a linear framework. The two-stage hurdle approach also improved on the baseline, demonstrating its ability to address the zero-inflated structure of the data.

Key modeling challenge

The outage distribution has a heavy right tail: most observations are zero or near-zero, but storm events push county-level counts into the thousands. An RMSE objective penalizes these extremes heavily, yet all LSTM variants underestimate sharp peaks exceeding 2,000 in the highest-risk counties, which is expected behavior for conditional-mean estimators. This is an inherent limitation of MSE-trained models on heavy-tailed data.

Generator allocation

As an academic decision-support exercise, the team designed a greedy allocation framework to illustrate how forecast outputs could inform pre-storm resource deployment. Given five backup generators (each supporting up to 1,000 households), the framework iteratively assigns each generator to the county with the highest marginal predicted demand. Two demand definitions were explored:

  • Peak demand: maximum predicted hourly outage over the forecast window, prioritizing the single worst hour.
  • Cumulative demand: sum of predicted outages over 48 hours, prioritizing sustained exposure over time.

Both strategies agreed on four of the five generator placements, indicating that the highest-risk counties are robust targets regardless of how demand is defined. The cumulative approach was identified as the more appropriate framing for emergency generator deployment, where sustained coverage matters more than responding to a single peak hour.
